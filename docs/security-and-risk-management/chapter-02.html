Chapter 2: Risk Management

Overview of Risk Management​

  • Risk Management Process: Identifying, assessing, and reducing risk to acceptable levels while ensuring it stays controlled.
  • Key Components: Framing, assessing, responding to, and monitoring risks.
  • Risk Calculation: Risk = Threat × Vulnerability × Impact.

Risk Management Tiers (NIST SP 800-39)​

  • Tier 1 - Organizational View: Focuses on risk to the organization as a whole and sets risk tolerance.
  • Tier 2 - Business/Mission Process View: Focuses on major organizational functions and critical information flows.
  • Tier 3 - Information Systems View: Addresses risks specific to information systems but must align with higher-level risk considerations【5†source】 .

Risk Management Policy Components​

  • Key Objectives: Formal risk identification process, connection to strategic goals, risk tolerance, and internal controls.
  • Senior Leadership Role: Direct involvement in risk decisions, funding, and ensuring policies align with security and business goals【5†source】.

Risk Assessment Process​

  1. Risk Framing: Defines the context, assumptions, and priorities.
  2. Risk Assessment: Identifies vulnerabilities and threats, assesses their likelihood, and quantifies their potential impact.
  3. Risk Response: Involves decisions on risk mitigation, transference, acceptance, or avoidance【5†source】 .

Risk Mitigation Strategies​

  • Mitigation: Implement controls to reduce risk.
  • Transference: Shift the risk to another party (e.g., insurance).
  • Acceptance: Take no action, understanding the risks.
  • Avoidance: Stop risky activities entirely【5†source】 .

Risk Analysis Approaches​

  • Quantitative Risk Analysis: Assigns monetary values to risks and controls for decision-making (e.g., SLE × ARO = ALE).
  • Qualitative Risk Analysis: Uses experience and judgment to assess risks without precise numbers .

Vulnerabilities and Threats​

  • Types of Threats: Physical damage, human interaction, equipment failure, external and internal attacks, misuse, and software errors.
  • Critical Focus: Identifying high-priority risks to business continuity and minimizing business disruption .

Supply Chain Risk Management​

  • Integration: Supply chain risks are managed similarly to internal risks but require unique assessments of external vendors.
  • Examples: The Target breach in 2013 highlights risks from vendor networks being exploited .

Business Continuity and Risk Management​

  • Business Impact Analysis (BIA): Essential for identifying critical processes and IT resources to prioritize in risk mitigation.
  • Goal: Ensure continued operation of critical business functions in the event of a disruption .

Continuous Monitoring and Reporting​

  • Monitoring: Ongoing assessment of risk controls and response to changing threats or environments.
  • Risk Reporting: Uses visual tools like heat maps to provide risk visibility to senior leaders for strategic adjustments .

Key Risk Management Frameworks​

  • NIST RMF: Widely used in federal organizations, it provides a detailed step-by-step process for managing risks .
